Environmental Impact & Resource Justice

The Hidden Cost of a Query

Case Study: AI Data Centers in the Philadelphia-Delaware Region

Source Documents

  • 📄 Case & Mechanism Explanation
  • 📄 Field Guide Summary
Researchers: Mya Brown Cydney Young · Dwyer School of Nursing

The Case

Data centers — massive buildings housing thousands of computers — gather, process, and store vast amounts of digital information. They exist wherever massive electric power grids exist, which often means densely populated communities like those in the Philadelphia-Delaware region. These electric grids run on fossil fuels, the leading cause of pollution that impacts air quality and community health.

One specific example: a proposed 1.2 gigawatt data center in Delaware City would consume approximately 12.7 million gallons of water annually and rely on 516 diesel-powered generators. While promoted as economic progress, these facilities impose significant burdens on surrounding communities.

The Energy Problem

The energy demands of AI are staggering. A single ChatGPT query takes approximately ten times the energy of a standard search engine query. Data centers require the electricity equivalent of thousands of houses. Citizens in the Philadelphia area have already seen increases in electric bills as grid demand grows to power both everyday needs and expanding data center infrastructure.

The Water Problem

Data centers use massive amounts of water to cool and humidify servers. This affects communities across Pennsylvania and Delaware, increasing costs and reducing accessibility to water. The students frame this starkly: water is being used to keep technology alive rather than to keep humans alive and aid them.

Transparency and Economic Justice

Data centers operate as "black boxes" — citizens do not know how many exist in their state, how much energy they consume, what these facilities cost the community, or have any control over where they are built. Meanwhile, the corporations building them receive large tax breaks, create few permanent local jobs, and impose costs on communities while providing little in return.

The Moral Challenge

The case raises fundamental questions of fairness, harm, and distributive justice. Locals experience strain on water supplies, higher electricity costs, and increased air pollution, yet they do not share equitably in the benefits of AI development. When corporations utilize large amounts of natural resources without ensuring stability or compensation for affected communities, these practices violate principles of fairness and responsibility.

Citizen Responsibility

The students also reflect on shared responsibility: regular people enjoy technology without understanding how it works, what it costs, or how it impacts their communities. They argue citizens should be more informed and involved before subscribing to new AI services and platforms.

The moral challenge is finding a balance between innovation, fairness, and environmental responsibility — ensuring that the communities bearing the costs of AI infrastructure also share in its benefits.
